# Depth of Knowledge Rubric (Adapted for Checkpoint Selections)

**Source**: Webb, N. L. (2002). *Depth-of-knowledge levels for four content areas*. Wisconsin Center for Education Research. As operationalized by Yang, M., et al. (2025, Appendix A).

**Purpose**: To code the cognitive complexity of each participant-initiated prompt in the Claude baseline condition and each checkpoint selection in the agentic AI condition using Webb's four-level Depth of Knowledge taxonomy.

**Use**: Applied post-hoc by two independent coders. Each prompt (Claude condition) or checkpoint selection (agentic AI condition) is assigned one DoK level from 1 to 4. Inter-rater reliability is reported using Cohen's kappa.

**Unit of analysis**: One coding decision per prompt (Claude) or per checkpoint selection (agentic AI).

---

## Adapted Rubric (from Yang et al., 2025, Appendix A)

### Level 1: Recall and Recognition of Factual Information

- Prompts or selections that require basic recall of facts, definitions, or simple procedures.
- Low cognitive demand; straightforward and typically single-step queries or selections.
- Little to no follow-up or deeper engagement with AI responses or checkpoint alternatives.

**Example (Claude condition)**: "What is the Technology Acceptance Model?"

**Example (agentic AI condition)**: Selecting the first-listed option at a checkpoint without exploring alternatives.

### Level 2: Skills and Concepts Processing

- Prompts or selections that require comprehension and processing of information from AI outputs or checkpoint alternatives.
- Involve more than one step but still relatively straightforward.
- Some follow-up questions show an attempt to build on previous responses or checkpoint context.

**Example (Claude condition)**: "Can you explain how TAM's constructs relate to each other in an AI tool adoption study?"

**Example (agentic AI condition)**: Selecting an option at a checkpoint after comparing at least two alternatives on at least one named criterion.

### Level 3: Strategic Thinking and Reasoning

- Prompts or selections that require reasoning, planning, and using evidence.
- More complex and abstract, often involving multiple steps and deeper analysis.
- Follow-up questions build on previous interactions to explore topics more deeply.

**Example (Claude condition)**: "Given that TAM emphasizes perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use, what would be the implications of using it for a study on nontraditional students who have both positive and negative prior experiences with technology?"

**Example (agentic AI condition)**: Selecting a non-modal option at a checkpoint with an articulated rationale that references the research context, after considering at least two alternatives on multiple criteria.

### Level 4: Extended Thinking

- Prompts or selections that require complex reasoning, planning, developing, and evaluating.
- Involve multiple steps and sustained effort over a longer period.
- Follow-up questions show sophisticated development and refinement of ideas.

**Example (Claude condition)**: A sequence of prompts that progressively develops a novel theoretical framework by integrating constructs from multiple theories and evaluating the integration against the research context.

**Example (agentic AI condition)**: A sequence of checkpoint selections that deliberately departs from modal recommendations at multiple critical junctures to construct a coherent non-modal research design, with articulated rationale at each juncture.

---

## Adaptation Notes

The original Yang et al. (2025) rubric was developed for ChatGPT prompts in instructional design coursework. The present adaptation adjusts the rubric for two purposes:

1. **Checkpoint selections as coding units**: In the agentic AI condition, the analogue of a prompt is a checkpoint selection. A checkpoint selection is coded at the DoK level corresponding to the cognitive work evident in the selection rationale (think-aloud during the selection plus any follow-up query).

2. **Research-design context**: Example prompts are drawn from research methodology rather than general instructional design, matching the present study's scenarios.

## Coder Training and Reliability

Two independent coders review the rubric and code a pilot set of 20 prompts and 20 checkpoint selections (10 of each from pilot participants). Inter-rater reliability is computed as Cohen's kappa. The target is kappa >= 0.70. If reliability is below this threshold, coders meet to reconcile differences, refine rubric examples, and re-code until reliability is achieved. Final study coding proceeds only after reliability is established. Disagreements on study artifacts are resolved through adjudication by the Principal Investigator.

## Reporting

DoK levels are reported per participant per condition as descriptive statistics (mean, median, distribution across levels). Paper 2 foregrounds the between-condition contrast; Paper 3 uses DoK values as numeric descriptors within each case narrative.
