# Originality Rubric (Adapted)

**Source**: Yang, M., et al. (2025, Appendix A). British Journal of Educational Technology.

**Purpose**: To code the degree to which the final research design artifact reflects the participant's own intellectual contribution relative to AI-generated content. Measures whether the participant uses the AI as a tool to enhance original thinking or as a replacement for it.

**Use**: Applied post-hoc by two independent coders to the final research design artifact produced in each condition (Claude baseline: Google Doc artifact from Phase 0; agentic AI: final artifact from Phase 2 or Phase 3, depending on analytic focus). Each artifact receives one overall rating from 1 to 4. Inter-rater reliability is reported as Cohen's kappa.

**Unit of analysis**: One rating per condition per participant.

---

## Adapted Rubric (from Yang et al., 2025, Appendix A)

### Level 1: Low Originality

- The topic idea and research design are purely from the AI system (Claude or agentic AI).
- Direct copying and pasting from AI output without modification.
- Minimal to no personal input or original content.
- The participant's contribution is reliance on AI responses with little evidence of independent thinking.

### Level 2: Limited Originality

- The selection of topic and design ideas is generated based on the interaction with the AI but not substantially altered.
- Some modification of AI output but still heavily reliant on AI-generated content.
- Limited personal input and minimal original content.
- Basic attempts to paraphrase or slightly modify AI responses are visible.

### Level 3: Moderate Originality

- Topic idea is from the participant but significantly refined or adjusted based on interaction with the AI.
- Significant modification of AI output with substantial personal input.
- Integration of personal ideas, analysis, and creativity alongside AI contributions.
- Effective use of the AI as a tool to support and enhance original work.

### Level 4: High Originality

- The participant has a clear research idea from the beginning and uses the AI strategically to refine and enhance rather than generate original content.
- Extensive and sophisticated use of the AI to refine and enhance original content.
- High degree of personal input, creativity, and critical thinking.
- Consistent and purposeful prompting and use of the AI to obtain desired outputs that support the participant's own intellectual direction.

---

## Coding Procedure

1. Coders read the final research design artifact produced by the participant in each condition.
2. Coders review the corresponding chat transcript (Claude) or decision audit and think-aloud data (agentic AI) to assess which elements of the artifact originated from the AI versus from the participant.
3. Coders assign a rating from 1 to 4 using the rubric descriptors.
4. Coders document the primary evidence supporting the rating (e.g., specific passages that indicate the level of originality).

## Adaptation Notes

The original Yang et al. (2025) rubric was developed for instructional design projects in higher education courses. The present adaptation preserves the rubric structure exactly, with two contextual adjustments:

1. **Agentic AI applicability**: The rubric applies equally to checkpoint-governed agentic AI and to standard generative AI. Originality is coded relative to the AI's contribution in either case, with the understanding that the agentic AI presents multiple alternatives at checkpoints rather than a single response, changing the form but not the substance of what "AI-generated content" means for this rubric.

2. **Research-design artifacts**: The rubric is applied to research design documents rather than instructional design projects. Personal input is assessed in terms of research question framing, theoretical integration, methodological reasoning, and contextual adaptation rather than instructional design creativity.

## Coder Training and Reliability

Two independent coders review the rubric and rate a pilot set of 10 artifacts (5 Claude condition, 5 agentic AI condition) from pilot participants. Inter-rater reliability is computed as Cohen's kappa, with a target of kappa >= 0.70. Discrepancies are reconciled through discussion before study artifacts are coded.

## Reporting

Originality ratings are reported per participant per condition as a 1-4 ordinal score. Paper 2 foregrounds the between-condition contrast; Paper 3 uses ratings as numeric descriptors within each case narrative.

## Note on Yang et al.'s (2025) Finding

In the Yang et al. N = 73 sample, originality showed a non-significant negative correlation with learning performance (r = -0.13, p = 0.26). The authors interpreted this as suggesting that high-performing students in instructional design coursework relied more, not less, on ChatGPT outputs. The present study does not prejudge this finding for doctoral research design, and reports originality descriptively per case without inferential interpretation given N = 5.
