# Prompt Relevance and Evolution Rubric (Adapted)

**Source**: Yang, M., et al. (2025, Appendix A). British Journal of Educational Technology.

**Purpose**: To code the coherence and progression of sequential prompts (Claude condition) or checkpoint selections (agentic AI condition) across a session. Measures the extent to which each query builds on previous turns and moves the reasoning forward.

**Use**: Applied post-hoc by two independent coders, at the session level rather than the individual prompt level. Each session receives one overall rating from 1 to 4. Inter-rater reliability is reported as Cohen's kappa.

**Unit of analysis**: One rating per condition per participant (two ratings per participant total).

---

## Adapted Rubric (from Yang et al., 2025, Appendix A)

### Level 1: Low Relevance and Evolution

- Prompts or selections are largely unrelated to each other.
- Little to no narrowing of scope; queries are broad and disconnected.
- No clear progression or development in the line of questioning.
- Participant jumps between unrelated topics or aspects of the research design task.

### Level 2: Limited Relevance and Limited Evolution

- Prompts or selections show some relevance to each other but lack depth and focus.
- Limited narrowing of scope; some prompts build on previous ones but not consistently.
- Minimal progression in the complexity or specificity of questions.
- Some evidence of working through the task, but disjointed.

### Level 3: Moderate Relevance and Clear Evolution

- Prompts or selections are mostly relevant and build on each other with a clear line of questioning.
- Noticeable narrowing of scope; prompts become more focused and detailed over the session.
- Clear progression in the complexity and specificity of questions.
- Participant visibly works through the task by building on prior turns.

### Level 4: High Relevance and Advanced Evolution

- Prompts or selections are highly relevant and show a strong development of ideas.
- Clear narrowing of scope with highly focused and interrelated prompts.
- Advanced progression in the complexity, specificity, and depth of questions.
- Participant strategically refines queries to progressively develop the research design, iteratively integrating AI responses or checkpoint alternatives into a coherent whole.

---

## Adaptation Notes

The original Yang et al. (2025) rubric was developed for ChatGPT prompts. The present adaptation makes two adjustments:

1. **Agentic AI checkpoint sequences**: In the agentic AI condition, the analogue of a prompt sequence is the sequence of checkpoint selections plus any participant-initiated queries between checkpoints. Relevance and evolution are coded over this combined sequence.

2. **Research-design task context**: The rubric language is adjusted from instructional design to research design, where "narrowing of scope" refers to progressively specifying the research question, theoretical framework, sample, and method rather than general instructional design elements.

## Coder Training and Reliability

Two independent coders review the rubric and rate a pilot set of 10 sessions (5 Claude condition, 5 agentic AI condition) from pilot participants. Inter-rater reliability is computed as Cohen's kappa, with a target of kappa >= 0.70. Discrepancies are reconciled through discussion before study artifacts are coded.

## Reporting

Prompt relevance and evolution ratings are reported per participant per condition as a 1-4 ordinal score. Paper 2 foregrounds the between-condition contrast; Paper 3 uses ratings as numeric descriptors within each case narrative.

## Relationship to Yang et al.'s (2025) Findings

Yang and colleagues reported a strong positive correlation between prompt relevance and learning performance (r = 0.42, p < 0.01) in their N = 73 sample. The present N = 5 study cannot test correlation inferentially but reports the descriptive pattern per case and uses the rating as one data stream in the cross-case analysis.
